# ARIA Dashboard — Technical Specification

**Version:** 1.0  
**Date:** March 2026  
**Status:** Draft for internal review  
**Audience:** Engineering, data science, product

---

## 1. Overview

### 1.1 What we are building

A single-page web dashboard that displays the AI Risk Impact Assessment (ARIA) for every job role in CPFB's organisation. The dashboard scores each role on two dimensions — Automation Impact Score (AIS) and Augmentation Potential Score (APS) — and classifies it into one of nine strategic categories using the ARIA classification matrix.

### 1.2 What we are NOT building (Phase 2)

- Skills adjacency or gap analysis
- Role transition simulator or pathways
- Developmental pathways
- Scenario modelling
- Intelligence Studio (conversational AI)

### 1.3 Users

- CPFB HR leadership (primary): enterprise-wide view of workforce AI exposure
- CPFB department heads: filtered view of their department's roles
- CPFB analysts: per-role drill-down with full score breakdowns

```
    │
    ▼
[4] ARIA classification (see Section 4)
    │
    ▼
[5] Output: scored role dataset → dashboard
```

## 3. Scoring algorithm

### 3.1 Automation Impact Score (AIS)

The AIS answers: *how much of what this role does every day could AI or automation technology do instead?*

#### 3.1.1 Variables

| # | Variable | Weight | Inverse | Description |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| V1 | Cognitive routine level | 25% | No | Degree to which the role's cognitive tasks follow predictable, rule-based patterns. High = highly routine, repetitive mental work. |
| V2 | Data dependency and structuredness | 20% | No | Extent to which the role works with structured, digital, machine-readable data. High = heavily data-dependent with structured inputs. |
| V3 | Process repeatability and volume | 20% | No | Degree to which the role's processes are standardised, repeatable, and high-volume. High = highly standardised processes. |
| V4 | Social perception | 15% | Yes | Degree to which the role requires reading social cues, empathy, negotiation, or interpersonal nuance. High raw score = high social requirement = harder to automate. |
| V5 | Physical complexity | 10% | Yes | Degree to which the role requires physical presence, dexterity, or environmental interaction. High raw score = high physical requirement = harder to automate. |
| V6 | Regulatory accountability | 10% | Yes | Degree to which the role carries personal regulatory, legal, or fiduciary accountability. High raw score = high accountability = harder to automate. |

#### 3.1.2 Scoring each variable

Each variable is scored on a scale of 0–100 using a structured rubric. The scoring approach is LLM-assisted with human review:

**Step 1: Task extraction**
From the role description + O*NET mapping, extract a list of discrete tasks performed by the role.

**Step 2: Per-variable assessment**
For each variable, assess the role against the rubric using the task list as evidence. The LLM (Claude or GPT-4) is prompted with:

```
Given the following role and its tasks:
Role: {role_title}
Tasks: {task_list}

Score the following variable on a scale of 0-100:
Variable: {variable_name}
Definition: {variable_definition}

Scoring rubric:
- 0-20: Minimal presence of this characteristic
- 21-40: Low presence; occasional instances
- 41-60: Moderate presence; regular but not dominant
- 61-80: High presence; dominant characteristic of the role
- 81-100: Very high presence; defines the role

Provide:
1. Score (integer 0-100)
2. Justification (2-3 sentences citing specific tasks)
```

**Step 3: Human review**
A Pulsifi analyst reviews all LLM-generated scores, adjusting where the justification is weak or the score appears miscalibrated. All adjustments are logged with rationale.

#### 3.1.3 Inverse transformation

For variables V4, V5, V6 (inverse variables), the raw score is inverted before weighting:

```
adjusted_score = 100 - raw_score
```

Rationale: A role with high social perception requirements (raw score 80) is harder to automate, so the adjusted score (20) reflects low automation potential on that dimension.

#### 3.1.4 Composite AIS calculation

```
AIS = (V1_score × 0.25)
    + (V2_score × 0.20)
    + (V3_score × 0.20)
    + ((100 - V4_raw) × 0.15)
    + ((100 - V5_raw) × 0.10)
    + ((100 - V6_raw) × 0.10)
```

**Worked example: Customer Service Officer**

| Variable | Raw score | Inverse applied | Weight | Weighted score |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cognitive routine level | 78 | — | 25% | 19.5 |
| Data dependency | 72 | — | 20% | 14.4 |
| Process repeatability | 80 | — | 20% | 16.0 |
| Social perception | 42 | 100 - 42 = 58 | 15% | 8.7 |
| Physical complexity | 18 | 100 - 18 = 82 | 10% | 8.2 |
| Regulatory accountability | 22 | 100 - 22 = 78 | 10% | 7.8 |
| **AIS** | | | | **74.6** |

#### 3.1.5 AIS rating tiers

| AIS range | Rating | Strategic implication |
|---|---|---|
| 75–100 | Very high | Critical exposure — immediate intervention required |
| 60–74 | High | Transition required — structured reskilling within 12 months |
| 45–59 | Moderate | Monitor — quarterly review and contingency planning |
| 30–44 | Low | Augmentation focus — invest in AI tools, not transition |
| 0–29 | Very low | Minimal disruption — protected in near term |

### 3.2 Augmentation Potential Score (APS)

The APS answers: *to what degree can AI amplify what the human in this role produces, without displacing them?*

#### 3.2.1 Variables

| # | Variable | Weight | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| V1 | Knowledge and information processing intensity | 25% | Volume and complexity of information the role must synthesise, analyse, or produce. High = heavy knowledge work. |
| V2 | Output quality and volume sensitivity | 25% | Degree to which the role's outputs benefit from higher volume or improved quality. High = significant gains from AI-assisted output. |
| V3 | Decision support requirements | 20% | Extent to which the role makes or supports complex decisions that benefit from data analysis. High = heavy decision support needs. |
| V4 | Repetitive cognitive work within expert role | 20% | Amount of repetitive analytical or cognitive tasks within an otherwise expert-level role. High = lots of repetitive expert work AI could handle. |
| V5 | Communication and collaboration volume | 10% | Volume of communication, coordination, and collaboration the role manages. High = heavy communication load. |

#### 3.2.2 Scoring approach

Same LLM-assisted + human review process as AIS (Section 3.1.2). No inverse transformations — all APS variables are scored directly (higher = more augmentation potential).

#### 3.2.3 Composite APS calculation

```
APS = (V1_score × 0.25)
    + (V2_score × 0.25)
    + (V3_score × 0.20)
    + (V4_score × 0.20)
    + (V5_score × 0.10)
```

**Worked example: Policy Research Officer**

| Variable | Score | Weight | Weighted score |
|---|---|---|---|
| Knowledge and information processing intensity | 82 | 25% | 20.5 |
| Output quality and volume sensitivity | 76 | 25% | 19.0 |
| Decision support requirements | 68 | 20% | 13.6 |
| Repetitive cognitive work within expert role | 72 | 20% | 14.4 |
| Communication and collaboration volume | 64 | 10% | 6.4 |
| **APS** | | | **73.9** |

#### 3.2.4 APS rating tiers

| APS range | Rating | Strategic implication |
|---|---|---|
| 75–100 | Very high | Immediate AI tool deployment — highest productivity return |
| 60–74 | High | Strong augmentation case — structured AI adoption within 6 months |
| 45–59 | Moderate | Selective augmentation — identify specific high-value AI tools |
| 30–44 | Low | Limited opportunity — monitor as AI capabilities evolve |
| 0–29 | Very low | Minimal augmentation potential in current AI landscape |

## 4. ARIA classification matrix

### 4.1 Matrix definition

The ARIA classification combines AIS and APS into a 3×3 matrix. The five-tier ratings (Section 3.1.5, 3.2.4) collapse into three bands for the matrix:

| Band | AIS range | APS range |
|---|---|---|
| Low | 0–44 | 0–44 |
| Medium | 45–64 | 45–64 |
| High | 65–100 | 65–100 |

### 4.2 Classification lookup

| | AIS Low (0–44) | AIS Medium (45–64) | AIS High (65–100) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **APS High (65–100)** | Expand | Optimize | Transform |
| **APS Medium (45–64)** | Invest selectively | Adapt | Accelerate |
| **APS Low (0–44)** | Maintain | Monitor | Transition |

### 4.3 Classification definitions and recommendations

| Classification | Priority | Definition | Recommended action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Transform | Critical | High automation risk AND high augmentation potential. Highest impact zone. | Radical role redesign. Role evolves into AI orchestration function. Timeline: 6–12 months. |
| Accelerate | High | High automation risk, moderate augmentation. Displacement likely if no action. | Begin role restructuring. Deploy AI aggressively to mitigate risk, redesign around AI strengths. |
| Transition | High | High automation risk, low augmentation. AI replaces most of the role's value. | Plan transition to adjacent roles via structured reskilling. Identify target roles with skills overlap. |
| Optimize | Medium | Moderate automation risk, high augmentation. Good risk-reward trade-off. | Scale AI-human teams. Shift role toward high-judgment tasks before automation erodes routine components. |
| Adapt | Medium | Moderate automation risk and augmentation. Balanced position. | Integrate AI into existing workflows. Train employees on AI collaboration. |
| Monitor | Low-medium | Moderate automation risk, low augmentation. Some routine tasks at risk. | Apply AI for selective tasks without full role overhaul. Review quarterly. |
| Expand | Low | Low automation risk, high augmentation. Best-case scenario. | Embed AI tools to expand role scope and impact. Opportunity to scale up human interactions. |
| Invest selectively | Low | Low automation risk, moderate augmentation. Stable with pockets of opportunity. | Identify specific efficiency gains. No urgency for structural change. |
| Maintain | Minimal | Low automation risk, low augmentation. Human judgment essential. | Retain status quo. Role is protected in the near-to-medium term. |

### 4.4 Boundary handling

Roles scoring within 3 points of a band boundary (e.g., AIS = 43 or AIS = 66) are flagged as "boundary cases" in the dashboard and include a note that small scoring adjustments could shift the classification. This is important for transparency — CPFB analysts should know when a classification is firmly in its zone versus sitting on the edge.

### 4.5 Algorithm pseudocode

```python
def classify_role(role):
    """
    Input: role dict with 'ais' and 'aps' composite scores
    Output: classification string + metadata
    """
    ais = role['ais']
    aps = role['aps']

    # Band assignment
    ais_band = 'low' if ais <= 44 else ('medium' if ais <= 64 else 'high')
    aps_band = 'low' if aps <= 44 else ('medium' if aps <= 64 else 'high')

    # Classification lookup
    matrix = {
        ('low',  'high'):   'Expand',
        ('medium','high'):  'Optimize',
        ('high', 'high'):   'Transform',
        ('low',  'medium'): 'Invest selectively',
        ('medium','medium'):'Adapt',
        ('high', 'medium'): 'Accelerate',
        ('low',  'low'):    'Maintain',
        ('medium','low'):   'Monitor',
        ('high', 'low'):    'Transition',
    }
    classification = matrix[(ais_band, aps_band)]

    # Boundary flag
    ais_boundary = min(abs(ais - 44), abs(ais - 65)) <= 3
    aps_boundary = min(abs(aps - 44), abs(aps - 65)) <= 3
    is_boundary = ais_boundary or aps_boundary

    # Risk level
    risk = 'Very high' if ais >= 75 else (
           'High' if ais >= 60 else (
           'Moderate' if ais >= 45 else (
           'Low' if ais >= 30 else 'Very low')))

    return {
        'classification': classification,
        'ais_band': ais_band,
        'aps_band': aps_band,
        'risk_level': risk,
        'is_boundary_case': is_boundary,
    }
```

## 5. Dashboard specification

### 5.1 Architecture

```
┌─────────────┐     ┌──────────────┐     ┌───────────────┐
│  Data layer  │────▶│  API layer   │────▶│  Frontend     │
│  (Postgres)  │     │  (FastAPI)   │     │  (React)      │
└─────────────┘     └──────────────┘     └───────────────┘
```

**Data layer:** PostgreSQL database storing scored role data, variable breakdowns, and classification metadata.

**API layer:** FastAPI service exposing endpoints for the dashboard. For a one-off analysis, this can be simplified to a static JSON file served directly if CPFB doesn't need real-time updates.

**Frontend:** React single-page application with Tailwind CSS for styling and Chart.js or Recharts for visualisations.

### 5.2 Data model

```sql
-- Core tables
CREATE TABLE departments (
    id          SERIAL PRIMARY KEY,
    name        VARCHAR(100) NOT NULL,
    headcount   INTEGER
);

CREATE TABLE roles (
    id              SERIAL PRIMARY KEY,
    title           VARCHAR(200) NOT NULL,
    role_code       VARCHAR(50) UNIQUE,
    department_id   INTEGER REFERENCES departments(id),
    grade           VARCHAR(50),
    headcount       INTEGER DEFAULT 1,
    description     TEXT,
    onet_code       VARCHAR(20),  -- O*NET SOC mapping if available
    ais_composite   DECIMAL(5,1),
    aps_composite   DECIMAL(5,1),
    classification  VARCHAR(30),
    risk_level      VARCHAR(20),
    is_boundary     BOOLEAN DEFAULT FALSE,
    scored_at       TIMESTAMP DEFAULT NOW(),
    reviewed_by     VARCHAR(100),
    review_status   VARCHAR(20) DEFAULT 'pending'
        -- pending | reviewed | disputed | finalised
);

CREATE TABLE ais_variables (
    id          SERIAL PRIMARY KEY,
    role_id     INTEGER REFERENCES roles(id),
    variable    VARCHAR(50) NOT NULL,
        -- crl | dds | prv | sp | pc | ra
    raw_score   INTEGER CHECK (raw_score BETWEEN 0 AND 100),
    is_inverse  BOOLEAN DEFAULT FALSE,
    adjusted    INTEGER CHECK (adjusted BETWEEN 0 AND 100),
    weight      DECIMAL(3,2),
    weighted    DECIMAL(5,1),
    rationale   TEXT,
    source      VARCHAR(20) DEFAULT 'llm'
        -- llm | manual | hybrid
);

CREATE TABLE aps_variables (
    id          SERIAL PRIMARY KEY,
    role_id     INTEGER REFERENCES roles(id),
    variable    VARCHAR(50) NOT NULL,
        -- kip | oqv | dsr | rcw | ccv
    score       INTEGER CHECK (score BETWEEN 0 AND 100),
    weight      DECIMAL(3,2),
    weighted    DECIMAL(5,1),
    rationale   TEXT,
    source      VARCHAR(20) DEFAULT 'llm'
);

-- Audit trail for score adjustments
CREATE TABLE score_adjustments (
    id              SERIAL PRIMARY KEY,
    role_id         INTEGER REFERENCES roles(id),
    score_type      VARCHAR(3),  -- ais | aps
    variable        VARCHAR(50),
    old_score       INTEGER,
    new_score       INTEGER,
    adjusted_by     VARCHAR(100),
    reason          TEXT,
    adjusted_at     TIMESTAMP DEFAULT NOW()
);
```

## 6. Scoring engine implementation

### 6.1 Pipeline overview

```
For each role in CPFB dataset:
    │
    [1] Extract tasks from role description
    │   - Use LLM to decompose description into 5-15 discrete tasks
    │   - Map each task to O*NET IWA codes where possible
    │
    [2] Score 6 AIS variables
    │   - Prompt LLM with role + tasks + variable rubric
    │   - Collect score (0-100) + justification per variable
    │   - Apply inverse for V4, V5, V6
    │   - Calculate weighted composite
    │
    [3] Score 5 APS variables
    │   - Same LLM-assisted approach
    │   - No inversions
    │   - Calculate weighted composite
    │
    [4] Classify role
    │   - Apply ARIA matrix lookup
    │   - Flag boundary cases
    │
    [5] Human review queue
        - Analyst reviews all scores
        - Adjustments logged to audit trail
        - Status: pending → reviewed → finalised
```

### 6.2 LLM scoring prompt template

```
SYSTEM:
You are an AI workforce impact analyst. You assess job roles
for automation potential and AI augmentation potential using a
structured methodology. You must provide scores grounded in
specific tasks and responsibilities of the role.

USER:
## Role assessment

**Role:** {role_title}
**Department:** {department}
**Description:** {role_description}

**Extracted tasks:**
{numbered_task_list}

## Score the following variable

**Variable:** {variable_name}
**Definition:** {variable_definition}

**Scoring rubric:**
- 0-20:  Minimal — this characteristic is barely present in the role
- 21-40: Low — occasional instances, not a defining feature
- 41-60: Moderate — regularly present but balanced by other factors
- 61-80: High — dominant characteristic, significantly defines the role
- 81-100: Very high — the primary defining feature of the role

**Response format (JSON):**
{
  "score": <integer 0-100>,
  "confidence": <"high" | "medium" | "low">,
  "justification": "<2-3 sentences citing specific tasks>",
  "key_tasks": ["<task numbers most relevant to this score>"]
}
```

### 6.3 Calibration process

Before scoring the full population:

1. Select 5 pilot roles spanning different AIS/APS profiles (one very high AIS, one very low, one balanced, etc.)
2. Score all 11 variables for each pilot role using the LLM
3. Have 2 Pulsifi analysts independently score the same 5 roles manually
4. Compare LLM vs human scores — acceptable variance is +/- 10 points per variable
5. Where variance exceeds threshold, refine the prompt or rubric
6. Re-score pilot roles until LLM-human alignment is within tolerance
7. Present pilot results to CPFB for validation before scaling

### 6.4 Throughput estimate

Assuming ~150 distinct roles:
- 11 variables per role × 150 roles = 1,650 LLM calls
- At ~30 seconds per call (including prompt construction and parsing): ~14 hours compute time
- Human review at 5 minutes per role: ~12.5 hours analyst time
- Calibration and adjustments: ~20 hours
- Total: approximately 2-3 weeks for scoring phase with one analyst + one engineer

## 10. Methodology defensibility

### 10.1 Academic foundation

The ARIA framework draws on established task-based automation assessment methodologies from labour economics:

- Task decomposition approach: Autor, Levy & Murnane (2003); Acemoglu & Restrepo (2019)
- O*NET task taxonomy: US Department of Labor occupational database with 19,000+ task statements
- AI exposure scoring: Eloundou et al. (2024) — GPT-4 annotation of O*NET tasks for automation potential
- Automation vs augmentation distinction: Brynjolfsson (2022); Stanford WORKBank framework (2025)

### 10.2 Key defensibility points for CPFB review

- Every score is backed by a per-variable justification citing specific tasks in the role
- Full audit trail for any manual adjustments
- Methodology paper available on request (to be prepared as part of delivery)
- Pilot validation with CPFB before full-population scoring
- Boundary cases explicitly flagged — no false precision
- Override mechanism available — CPFB can adjust scores and see cascading impact

### 10.3 Known limitations (to be disclosed to CPFB)

- Scores reflect current-state AI capabilities; they will shift as technology evolves
- The framework assesses task-level automation potential, not actual displacement likelihood (which depends on organisational, economic, and regulatory factors)
- Weights (25/20/20/15/10/10 for AIS; 25/25/20/20/10 for APS) are calibrated against global research but not empirically validated against Singapore public sector outcomes specifically
- The 3-band thresholds (0-44 / 45-64 / 65-100) are pragmatic approximations; roles near boundaries should be treated as continuous scores, not discrete categories

## 11. Appendix: variable scoring rubrics

### 11.1 AIS variable rubrics

**V1: Cognitive routine level (weight: 25%)**

| Score range | Description | Example indicators |
|---|---|---|
| 0–20 | Tasks require novel problem-solving, creative judgment, or unprecedented decision-making in nearly every instance | Strategy formulation, crisis management, artistic creation |
| 21–40 | Mix of routine and non-routine cognitive work; some tasks follow patterns but significant judgment required | Project management, client advisory |
| 41–60 | Roughly equal split between routine and non-routine; clear procedures exist but exceptions are common | Mid-level analysis, team supervision |
| 61–80 | Majority of cognitive tasks follow established patterns with occasional exceptions | Data entry with validation, standard report generation |
| 81–100 | Nearly all cognitive tasks are rule-based, repetitive, and follow predictable patterns | Transaction processing, form validation, template-based correspondence |

**V2: Data dependency and structuredness (weight: 20%)**

| Score range | Description |
|---|---|
| 0–20 | Role primarily works with unstructured, qualitative, or tacit information |
| 21–40 | Mix of structured and unstructured data; significant interpretation required |
| 41–60 | Regular use of structured data but with substantial unstructured inputs |
| 61–80 | Heavy reliance on structured, digital data in standardised formats |
| 81–100 | Almost entirely dependent on structured, machine-readable data inputs |

**V3: Process repeatability and volume (weight: 20%)**

| Score range | Description |
|---|---|
| 0–20 | Each task instance is unique; no standardised process |
| 21–40 | Some recurring processes but with significant variation per instance |
| 41–60 | Moderate standardisation; processes are documented but adaptation is common |
